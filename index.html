Multiband Compression

An interactive lesson — split, squeeze, and glue your mix.

1. Quick refresher: threshold & ratio

A compressor turns down loud parts of a signal automatically. Two knobs do most of the work:

  • Threshold — the level (in dB) above which compression kicks in.
  • Ratio — how hard it squeezes. 4:1 means every 4 dB over the threshold becomes 1 dB at the output.

Straight line = no compression. The bend at the threshold shows the ratio squeezing everything above it.

2. Why split into bands?

A regular (single-band) compressor reacts to the whole signal. So a loud kick drum can duck the vocals, or a boomy bass note can pull the whole mix down for a second — known as pumping.

A multiband compressor first splits the signal into frequency bands (typically low, mid, and high) using crossover filters, then compresses each band independently, then sums them back together. 

  ┌──────── Low band ─────── Compressor ─┐
  │                                      │
  in ──┼──────── Mid band ─────── Compressor ─┼──► out
  │                                      │
  └──────── High band ─────── Compressor ─┘

The big idea: tame the thing that's too loud without touching everything else.

3. Crossover frequencies

The crossovers define where one band ends and the next begins. Two crossover points (low/mid and mid/high) give you three bands. Typical starting points:

  • Low / Mid crossover: 120 – 250 Hz (separates bass from body)
  • Mid / High crossover: 2 – 5 kHz (separates body from presence/air)

Drag the sliders. The three colored regions are what the low, mid, and high compressors each "see."

4. Per-band settings

Each band gets its own threshold, ratio, attack, release, and makeup gain. That's the whole point — you can compress boomy bass hard while leaving the mids alone, or tame harsh "s" sounds in the highs without dulling the body of a vocal.

5. What is it good for?

Mastering glue

Gentle compression (1.5:1 – 2:1, low threshold, slow attack) across all three bands to make a mix feel cohesive and loud without obvious pumping.

Taming boomy bass

Only the low band compresses hard. Sub bass notes that jump out get squashed, while the rest of the mix stays untouched.

De-essing

A narrow high band (say 5–9 kHz) with a fast attack and high ratio catches harsh "s" and "t" sounds without dulling the rest of the vocal.

Broadcast / loudness

Radio and podcast processors use multiband comp to keep everything at a consistent loudness regardless of whether the program is speech, music, or a mix of both.

6. Pitfalls

  • Over-squashing. Too much reduction on every band = flat, lifeless mix. Dynamics are good.
  • Pumping within a band. Short attack + short release on a kick-heavy low band can still pump audibly.
  • Phase smearing. Crossover filters introduce phase shifts. Usually inaudible with modern designs (Linkwitz–Riley), but stacking several multibands can add up.
  • Solving EQ problems with compression. If a frequency range is always too loud, EQ it. Compression is for dynamic problems.
  • Set-and-forget ratios. Every mix is different. The preset is a starting point, not the answer.
